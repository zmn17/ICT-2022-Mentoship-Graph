## What is Liquidity?

In ICT methodology, **liquidity** refers to resting orders (stop-losses and pending orders) that cluster at predictable price levels. The market is algorithmically driven to seek and "hunt" these pools of liquidity to fill large institutional orders.

---

## Two Types of Liquidity

### Sell-Side Liquidity (SSL)
- Resting **below** price lows
- Consists of retail traders' **buy stop-losses** and sell-stop orders
- Found below:
  - Relative equal lows
  - Short-term swing lows
  - Previous session lows
  - Trendline supports

### Buy-Side Liquidity (BSL)
- Resting **above** price highs
- Consists of retail traders' **sell stop-losses** and buy-stop orders
- Found above:
  - Relative equal highs
  - Short-term swing highs
  - Previous session highs
  - Trendline resistances

---

## Liquidity Hunts (Runs)

A **liquidity run** (or liquidity hunt) is when price intentionally sweeps through a liquidity level to trigger resting orders, then reverses. This is the **manipulation** phase of [Power of 3](power-of-3.md).

```
Price Action During a Sell-Side Liquidity Hunt:

     ────────────────  Short-term Low (relative equal lows)
           │
           ▼  Price sweeps below (MANIPULATION)
     ══════════════════  Sell-side liquidity taken
           │
           ▲  Price reverses sharply (DISTRIBUTION begins)
           │
     ────────────────  Rally toward buy-side targets
```

## Relative Equal Lows/Highs

**Relative equal lows** are two or more swing lows at approximately the same price level:

1. They are **visible** to retail traders who place stops just below
2. They create concentrated pools of liquidity
3. Algorithms specifically target these levels
4. A sweep of relative equal lows is a high-probability reversal signal

From Episode 9:
> "Liquidity builds below these relative equal lows, setting the stage for a retail trap where stops are taken out before rallying."

## Liquidity and Session Timing

| Session | Typical Liquidity Behavior |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Asia/London | Builds liquidity (accumulation) |
| London Open | May hunt Asian session highs/lows |
| NY Open (9:30 AM) | Often hunts London session extremes |
| NY Lunch (12:00–1:00 PM) | Builds new liquidity during consolidation |
| NY PM (1:00–3:00 PM) | Hunts lunch-hour liquidity for the true move |

## How to Trade Liquidity Hunts

### Setup Recognition
1. Identify **obvious liquidity** (equal lows/highs, session lows/highs)
2. Wait for price to **sweep** that level (take the stops)
3. Look for a reaction at an [order block](order-blocks.md) or [fair value gap](fair-value-gaps.md) after the sweep
4. Enter in the **opposite direction** of the hunt

### Key Rule from Episode 9:
> "Price typically does not squeeze beyond both the low of the day and the imbalances simultaneously, reflecting measured liquidity hunts."

Liquidity hunts are **measured** — algorithms take one pool at a time, not everything at once.

---

## Liquidity as Fuel

Think of liquidity as the **fuel** for price movement:
- Price moves **toward** liquidity (it needs opposing orders to fill)
- After taking liquidity, price moves toward the **next pool**
- The direction of the "true move" (distribution) is **away** from the hunted liquidity and **toward** the opposite side

---

## Sweep vs. Run (Ep 23)

Two ways price interacts with a high/low — the distinction matters for whether to fade or follow:

| Term | Behavior | Implication |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Sweep** | Price pokes just beyond the level, then **shallow-reverses** | Liquidity grab → **fade** it (reversal) |
| **Run** | Price trades **through and continues** | Genuine expansion → **follow** it (continuation) |

Reading which is happening (a sweep of an old high vs a run *through* it) tells you whether the level is a reversal point or a waypoint.
